A Charter for the Enlightening FAQ

Preface

This FAQ addresses practical questions that arise when people try to apply the Charter in real situations.

Where the Guidepost outlines boundaries and known failure modes, this document focuses on how those tensions tend to appear in practice.

Frequently Asked Questions

1. Who decides when someone has forfeited reciprocity or when triage applies?

No single person or authority.

The Charter is deliberately non-hierarchical. Scrutiny is distributed: any participant can identify patterns of bad faith, and these are assessed through open dialogue. In practice, persistent bad-faith actors tend to isolate themselves, as others disengage rather than escalate.

There is no central enforcement mechanism.

2. How is intervention authorised under the Protocols?

The Charter does not create new institutions.

It acts as a moral framework for existing actors—individuals, organisations, governments, and movements. Any intervention must withstand public Mirror Scrutiny and be justifiable against the Articles.

Historically, legitimate actions tend to remain open to scrutiny. Illegitimate actions rely on concealment.

3. Are “Conscious Actors” a new elite?

No.

The term is descriptive, not a title or rank. Anyone applying the Charter’s principles in practice can be considered a Conscious Actor. There is no certification, hierarchy, or governing body.

4. Doesn’t the private-sphere exception enable surveillance?

Only if privacy and secrecy are treated as the same thing.

The Charter distinguishes between them:

Privacy supports growth, reflection, and creation, and is protected.
Secrecy that enables harm or avoids accountability is subject to scrutiny.

A practical test: does the private space eventually produce something accountable or beneficial beyond itself?

5. Can intrinsic worth be unconditional while still containing people?

Yes.

Intrinsic worth is inalienable. It does not depend on behaviour, status, or participation.

However, access to certain shared spaces (such as public discourse or influence) can be limited when necessary. Containment, in this context, means disengagement from harmful patterns—not dehumanisation or punishment.

6. What about people who lack capacity (e.g. severe disability or mental illness)?

Their intrinsic worth remains unchanged.

Article VI (Duty to Empower) applies most strongly here. The aim is to support and expand whatever agency is possible, while removing barriers that limit participation or autonomy.

7. Is this just paternalism in another form?

No.

The Charter distinguishes between protection that disempowers and support that strengthens. Article VI prioritises enabling individuals to act for themselves, rather than shielding them through control or restriction.

Attempts to limit access to information or suppress participation in the name of “protection” conflict with this principle.

8. Why are the dialogues included as part of the work?

Because the Charter is intentionally not closed.

The text contains flexibility by design. The dialogues document how its principles were tested under pressure, including where easy or authoritarian answers were rejected.

They function as a record of application, not authority—helping to preserve transparency and ongoing scrutiny.

They also act as a safeguard against rigid interpretation.

Where the principles risk being simplified or applied mechanically, the dialogues reintroduce context, disagreement, and unresolved tension. This helps prevent the Charter from being reduced to fixed doctrine.
